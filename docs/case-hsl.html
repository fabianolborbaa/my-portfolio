03 · Healthcare · Mobile

Turning hospital care
into a trusted digital journey

A mobile hub that gave patients predictable, self-serve access to care — before, during, and after every touchpoint at one of Latin America's top hospitals.

Role Product Designer
Client Hospital Sírio-Libanês
Duration 12 months · 2021–2022
Focus Service Design · Mobile · IA
9:41
Open in full screen ↗
Context

Hospital Sírio-Libanês is one of Latin America's most important medical centres — a philanthropic institution in São Paulo serving around 120,000 patients per year, recognised for high-complexity treatments.

Despite the quality of clinical care, the patient experience was running on manual processes. Check-in required physical presence. Queues were unpredictable. Meal orders were paper-based. Exams and documents were scattered. For a patient already under stress, every friction point compounded the anxiety.

The brief was to build a patient app. The real job was something harder: design an experience worthy of the hospital's reputation — before, during, and after every touchpoint.

The problem

When friction compounds
anxiety

The hospital's clinical excellence didn't extend to the administrative experience. Three pressure points converged:

01

Check-in as a bottleneck

Manual front-desk processes created long queues and unpredictable wait times. Every patient had to physically register, re-enter data, and wait with no visibility into their status. For someone already anxious about a procedure, the queue was the worst possible start.

02

Disconnected touchpoints

Appointments, inpatient services, documents, and exam results lived in separate systems — or on paper. Patients navigated multiple channels for tasks that should have been one. Every reconnection point was a potential for error, frustration, or a call to front desk.

03

Operational cost of paper

Meal ordering, form handling, and document management were paper-based. The operational cost — in time, in staff effort, in rework — was significant. And the patient experience reflected it.

In a hospital, uncertainty isn't just a UX problem —
it's a care problem.

Anxiety affects how patients experience treatment. Predictability isn't a nice-to-have. It's part of the clinical mission.

The mission

Make patients protagonists
of their own health

The product mission — "Tornar as pessoas protagonistas da sua própria saúde" — was more than a tagline. It defined the design direction: not a service portal, not a hospital brochure, but a tool that gives patients genuine control over their own care journey.

🏥

What the hospital needed

Reduce front-desk volume, eliminate paper-based processes, and lower operational cost without compromising care quality.

🧑‍⚕️

What the patient needed

Predictability, clarity, and autonomy — knowing what's happening, what comes next, and how to act — without depending on a person at a desk.

The design direction

"Um ponto que acolhe, uma ponte que nutre relacionamentos" — a point that welcomes, a bridge that nurtures. The app needed to feel like the hospital extending its care into the patient's daily life, not like software bolted onto an administrative process.

Approach

Shipped in phases.
Prioritised by impact.

I recommended shipping fewer flows first — but with higher robustness at the most critical moments — rather than expanding scope quickly and creating inconsistency. The 12-month arc reflected that bet.

2021

Phase 01 — Reduce uncertainty

The foundation: target the highest-friction, highest-volume moments first. Pre check-in reduced desk time and eliminated repeated data entry. Real-time queue tracking made status and next steps transparent. Information architecture and navigation patterns lowered cognitive load across the whole app.

↓ 30% reception waiting time 600+ monthly appointments improved
2021–22

Phase 02 — Expand value

Once the foundation proved reliable, we expanded into new journey domains. Digital meal ordering gave inpatients direct control over their diet — replacing paper forms and manual adjustments. Remote care improved continuity across touchpoints. Centralised exam and document access eliminated the most common reason patients called the front desk.

Design decisions

Three decisions that
shaped everything

The brief was large enough to justify almost any direction. These three decisions defined which direction was worth defending.

01

Predictability as a product feature

Every critical state had an explicit label: "waiting to be called," "check-in completed," "your exam is ready." Strong confirmations on critical actions. Guidance centred on what the patient should do now, not on internal hospital processes.

The alternative we resisted

Generic status messages ("processing," "pending") that leave patients to interpret their own situation — and reach for the phone to ask a human instead.

02

Patient-task IA, not org-chart IA

Navigation was built around patient tasks and journey moments — before, during, after — not around the hospital's internal departments. Plain-language labels, action-oriented microcopy, fewer paths to the same action.

The alternative we resisted

A structure mirroring hospital departments — Cardiology, Nutrition, Administration — that makes sense internally but forces patients to understand the org to find what they need.

03

Guardrails that reduce rework

In-context instructions and validation before the error state. Clear hierarchy and unambiguous CTAs. Consistent patterns across features so that what patients learn in check-in transfers to meal ordering and exams.

The alternative we resisted

Error messages only at submission — which generates support calls, rework, and the kind of frustration that erodes trust faster than any design can rebuild it.

The features

Three features.
One journey.

Each feature addressed a specific moment of friction. Together, they covered the patient journey end to end.

01

Pre check-in — Before you arrive

Highest volume · Highest friction · Phase 01 priority

From home, patients open their appointment, submit their arrival info, and monitor the queue in real time. When they arrive at the hospital, check-in takes seconds — no paperwork, no repeated questions, no waiting in a line to talk to someone.

Queue tracking was designed around explicit state messages rather than vague progress bars. "You're 3rd in line. Estimated wait: 12 minutes." That specificity was a deliberate choice — it reduces the anxiety of not knowing, which is often worse than a longer wait.

The outcome

30% reduction in average reception waiting time. Over 600 additional monthly appointments handled without increased front-desk load.

02

Digital meal ordering — While you're here

Inpatient experience · Replaces paper process · Phase 02

As soon as a patient is admitted and entitled to hospital meals, the app surfaces a dedicated section: menus created by nutritionists, tailored to their care plan. Patients choose and modify their meals directly — no paper forms, no repeating orders to a staff member.

For a patient spending days in a hospital, this is not a small thing. Having control over something as basic as what you eat is a meaningful part of feeling like a person, not a case number.

The outcome

10,000+ digital meals ordered or modified. Significant reduction in paper consumption and staff time spent on manual meal adjustments.

03

Exams & Population Health — After you leave

Continuity of care · 500K+ lives · Phase 03

1.5 million exam results centralised in one place, accessible to patients securely. 3,000+ appointments booked without calling reception. The app became the patient's long-term relationship with the hospital — not just a visit companion.

Population Health (Saúde Populacional) extended the platform to corporate partnerships: companies with employee health plans managing 500,000+ lives could now access all their benefits through a single app, in digital or hybrid care models. Delivered in approximately 3 months.

The outcome

1.5M exams available in-app. 500K+ lives under management. 3,000+ appointments self-scheduled. The front desk became a last resort, not a first step.

What I learned

The real takeaways

→

In healthcare, predictability is part of care. Reducing uncertainty about what happens next isn't a UX nicety — it directly affects how patients experience their treatment and recover. This shaped every copy decision, every status label, every confirmation message.

→

Robustness at critical moments beats breadth of features. Shipping fewer flows with higher quality at high-stakes touchpoints (check-in, discharge, exam results) produced more durable trust than a wide but shallow feature set would have.

→

Org-chart IA is the enemy of patient-centred design. The pressure to structure navigation around internal departments is constant — stakeholders think in departments, not in patient tasks. Defending journey-based IA requires persistent advocacy, not just a good argument.

→

Operational efficiency and patient experience are the same problem. Every time we reduced a call to the front desk, we improved both the patient's experience and the hospital's operational cost. That alignment was the strongest argument for every design decision.
