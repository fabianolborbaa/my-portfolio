01 · Trust & Critical UX

Designing trust
at the worst moment

Turning a premium insurance benefit that forced anxious customers to call support into a clear, self-service flow — at scale.

Role Senior Product Designer
Industry Travel · Subscription
Focus UX Strategy · Service Design
Outcome Self-serve CFAR flow at scale
Context

A premium travel subscription offered a "Cancel for Any Reason" benefit — but when customers needed to use it, they had to call. At the worst possible moment, they were forced to relive the problem out loud, hold on the line, and hope the agent got it right.

The benefit was designed to give autonomy. In practice, it only felt reliable when a human mediated trust. That gap — between what the product promised and what it delivered under pressure — was the problem worth solving.

CFAR adoption was growing faster than the product could handle. The contact center load was disproportionate, each interaction inconsistent, and the cost of scaling through people alone was unsustainable.

The obvious answer

What everyone would have done

The instinct was clear: make the benefit more visible. Surface it on the booking confirmation. Push a notification when a flight gets delayed. Add a banner at the top of the app. More visibility, more adoption, better metrics.

Rejected approach
✕
Show the benefit to everyone, always

Increases visibility metrics. Also surfaces a cancellation option to users who never need it — adding cognitive noise at exactly the wrong moments.

✕
Optimise the call flow instead of replacing it

Reduces average handle time. Doesn't solve the core problem: customers shouldn't have to call at all. Scaling a broken experience is not design.

✕
Add more information to the benefit page

The problem wasn't lack of information. Customers weren't confused — they were scared. No amount of copy cures doubt at crunch time.

The real insight came from looking at what customers actually asked when they called. It wasn't "how does this work?" It was two simpler, scarier questions.

"Will this actually work?"  ·  "What happens after I cancel?"

Not policy questions. Certainty questions. The product had an information problem disguised as a trust problem.

The problem

Two pressures pulling
in the same direction

👤

From the customer's side

People calling weren't confused about the policy. They were scared it wouldn't work for them, specifically, right now. The call was a search for certainty — not information.

"Will this actually work?" "What happens after I cancel?"
📊

From the business's side

CFAR cancellations were generating disproportionate contact center volume. Adoption was growing faster than product maturity could handle.

  • Expensive to operate at any scale
  • Quality inconsistent across agents
  • Hard to scale without increasing friction
  • Reputational risk if the experience broke under load
Reframe

A benefit designed to give autonomy only felt reliable when someone mediated trust. The job wasn't to design a cancellation flow — it was to design certainty at scale.

The hard call

Choosing relevance
over visibility

The most contested decision in the project: don't show the benefit to users who can't use it. On the surface, this looks like hiding a premium feature. In practice, showing a cancellation option to an ineligible user creates false hope — which is worse than no option at all.

What growth wanted

Surface CFAR everywhere. More exposure = more perceived value = better subscription retention metrics.

vs
What the design did

Gate the flow on eligibility. Only eligible users see the option. The benefit only exists when it can actually be used.

Not every value needs to be pushed. Not every short-term metric is worth the reputational risk of promising something you can't deliver. This was the principle that shaped every screen that followed.

Design principles

Three decisions that
shaped the flow

Each principle came from rejecting a simpler path. The work was less about adding elements and more about removing the wrong incentives.

01

Relevance before visibility

The flow only exists for users who can actually use it. Ineligible users never see it.

Rejected: show to all, filter at confirmation

Creates false hope for ineligible users exactly when they need certainty most. A benefit that fails at the moment of use is worse than no benefit.

02

Answer doubt before the decision

Outcomes are made explicit before the user confirms. No surprises after the action.

Rejected: show outcome only on confirmation screen

Users in doubt don't convert. And users who convert without understanding the outcome generate more calls than those who didn't convert at all.

03

Treat the receipt as proof

The post-cancellation screen isn't a formality — it's the moment trust is confirmed or lost.

Rejected: standard confirmation + email follow-up

Email gets ignored at high-stress moments. The wallet credit needs to be visible and explicitly framed as "the benefit worked" — not buried in a receipt.

Design

The decisions, visible

Each screen carries a specific job. The goal wasn't a beautiful flow — it was a flow where nothing was left to interpretation.

Impact

What moved, and why

Each metric connects to a specific decision — not to the project as a whole. That's the difference between design that delivers and design that decorates.

−27.5% Cancellation-related calls Eligibility gate + pre-confirmation clarity removed the need to call
−9% Residual call volume Post-cancellation closure screen answered follow-up questions before they arose
+1.3 NPS at cancellation moment Users who cancelled felt the benefit worked — because the receipt said so, clearly
+8.2% Prime subscriptions Trust in the benefit at the worst moment translated into retention confidence
What I learned

The real takeaways

→

Trust is a product asset, not just a brand asset. The moment a user doubts whether a benefit will work, the benefit has already failed — regardless of whether it eventually delivers.

→

Not every optimisation should be visible. The best work here removed things — wrong incentives, unnecessary steps, premature information — rather than adding them.

→

Strong exception flows balance empathy and scale. You can't optimise for operational efficiency at the cost of feeling human. When it works, they compound.

→

Seniority shows up when design protects the business without sacrificing the customer. Pushing back on "more visibility" in favour of relevance was the hardest and most valuable call in the project.
