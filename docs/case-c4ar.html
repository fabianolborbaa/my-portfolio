01 · Trust & Critical UX

Designing trust
at the worst moment

Turning a premium insurance benefit that forced anxious customers to call support into a clear, self-service flow — at scale.

Role Senior Product Designer
Industry Travel · Subscription
Focus UX Strategy · Service Design
Outcome Self-serve CFAR flow at scale
Context

A premium travel subscription offered a "Cancel for Any Reason" benefit — but when customers needed to use it, they had to call. At the worst possible moment, they were forced to relive the problem out loud, hold on the line, and hope the agent got it right.

The benefit was designed to give autonomy. In practice, it only felt reliable when a human mediated trust. That gap — between what the product promised and what it delivered at crunch time — was the problem worth solving.

The Problem

Where customer anxiety
met operational cost

👤

From the customer's side

The most common questions weren't about policy details. They were about certainty. People weren't confused — they were scared.

"Will this actually work?" "What happens after I cancel?"
📊

From the business's side

CFAR cancellations were generating a disproportionate volume of contact center contacts. Adoption was growing faster than product maturity could handle.

  • Expensive to operate at scale
  • Inconsistent across agents
  • Hard to scale without increasing friction

A benefit designed to give autonomy only felt reliable when someone mediated trust. That's not a feature. That's a design debt.

Design Approach

Three principles that guided
every decision

The work was less about adding elements and more about removing the wrong incentives. Three pillars shaped the entire flow.

01

Relevance before visibility

The flow should only exist for people who could actually use it. Showing a benefit to ineligible users erodes trust faster than not showing it at all. Not every value needs to be pushed.

02

Reduce cognitive load at the moment of doubt

Provide the right information at the right time. Answer the key doubts before the confirmation step. Make outcomes predictable. Avoid post-action surprises. Reducing cognitive load protects both conversion and trust.

03

Close the loop after the action

The post-cancellation experience is not a receipt — it's the moment trust is either confirmed or lost. Treat wallet credit as proof the benefit worked. Factual confirmation, straightforward guidance, explicit next steps.

What I learned

The real takeaways

→

Trust is a product asset, not just a brand asset. The moment a user doubts whether a benefit will work, the benefit has already failed — regardless of whether it delivers.

→

Not every optimisation should be visible. The best UX work here removed things — wrong incentives, unnecessary steps, premature information — rather than adding them.

→

Strong exception flows balance empathy and scale. Designing for the worst moment requires both — you can't optimise for efficiency at the cost of feeling human.

→

Seniority shows up when design protects the business without sacrificing the customer. That's the hardest line to walk, and the most important one.
